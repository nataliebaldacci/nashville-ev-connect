Single Family Home

Detached house with dedicated parking

‚ñº

‚úì Good News!

Single-family homes have the simplest path to EV charging. Level 2 chargers are permitted in all residential zones under BL2025-1116.

Before You Start
Check your electrical panel capacity Required
Most Level 2 chargers need 40-50 amp circuit. Check if you have spare capacity or need upgrade.
Measure distance from panel to parking spot
Longer runs = higher cost. Ideal is <30 feet from panel to charger location.
Choose charger type (hardwired vs. plug-in)
Hardwired (NEMA 14-50) is most common. See charger comparison ‚Üí
Permits & Approvals
Get electrical permit from Metro Codes Required
Apply at epermits.nashville.gov. Fee: $75-150 depending on scope.
Schedule NES service upgrade (if needed) If Needed
If upgrading panel, contact NES for service requirements.
Installation
Hire licensed electrician
Must be TN-licensed. Get 2-3 quotes. Find contractors ‚Üí
Complete installation
Typically 2-4 hours for standard install. Panel upgrade adds 4-8 hours.
Pass electrical inspection Required
Metro Codes will inspect. Contractor usually schedules this.

Multifamily / Apartment

Rental apartments, townhomes with shared parking

‚ñº

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Considerations

Multifamily installations involve landlord/property manager approval, shared electrical infrastructure, and potentially zoning review for DCFC. Under BL2025-1116, DCFC in RM zones is limited to 7am-11pm.

Before You Start
Review lease for EV charging provisions Required
Check if lease allows modifications. Some newer leases include EV provisions.
Get written landlord/property manager approval Required
Request in writing. Include proposed location, charger specs, installation plan.
Assess electrical infrastructure
Building may need transformer upgrade for multiple chargers. Get electrician assessment.
Zoning & Permits
Verify zoning allows EV charging
Level 2: Permitted everywhere. DCFC: Check Parcel Viewer for zone.
Get electrical permit Required
Property owner or contractor applies at epermits.nashville.gov.
Check DCFC setback requirements DCFC Only
DCFC must be 20+ ft from adjacent residential zones. C-5 buffer may apply.
Metering & Billing
Decide on metering approach
Options: separate meter, sub-meter, included in rent, pay-per-use network.
Consider NES EVC rate schedule
EVC rate offers off-peak discounts. Requires separate meter.

Condominium / HOA

Owner-occupied units with HOA governance

‚ñº

‚ö†Ô∏è HOA Approval Required

Condo installations require board approval and may involve amending CC&Rs. Tennessee has no "right to charge" law requiring HOAs to allow EV charging. Start early and be patient!

HOA Process
Review CC&Rs and bylaws Required
Check for existing EV provisions or modification procedures. Note voting thresholds.
Submit written request to HOA board Required
Include: proposed location, charger specs, installation plan, liability/insurance info.
Attend board meeting / present proposal
Be prepared to address concerns: fire safety, electrical capacity, shared costs, appearance.
Get written approval from HOA Required
Keep copy of approval letter. Note any conditions or restrictions imposed.
Technical Assessment
Determine electrical source
Options: unit panel (if garage), common area panel, new sub-panel, dedicated transformer.
Get electrician load assessment
Building may need infrastructure upgrade if multiple owners want chargers. Consider future demand.
Coordinate with property management
They may have preferred contractors or need to provide access for installation.
Permits & Installation
Get electrical permit Required
Apply at epermits.nashville.gov. HOA approval letter may be required.
Complete installation
Use HOA-approved contractor if required. Coordinate timing with building management.
Pass inspection & notify HOA
Provide copy of inspection approval to HOA/property management for records.
