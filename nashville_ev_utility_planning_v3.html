1. Planning for EV Installation

Before installing EV charging infrastructure, property owners must assess their electrical system, choose appropriate charging equipment, and understand the utility and permitting requirements. The complexity of your project depends on the charger type and your existing electrical capacity.

‚ö†Ô∏è Supply Chain Considerations

Due to ongoing supply chain issues, transformers, poles, and meters may have 12-18 month lead times. NES reported approximately $80M in backorders as of 2024. Plan early and engage NES at least 30 days (residential) or 60 days (commercial) in advance.

Key Planning Steps

1

Assess Current Electrical System

Determine your building's existing electrical capacity and configuration:

  • Service Type: Single-phase (typical residential) or three-phase (commercial/multifamily)
  • Panel Capacity: Check amperage rating (e.g., 200A, 400A) and available spare capacity
  • Distance to Parking: Assess distance from panel to proposed charger location for conduit/wiring costs
2

Choose Charging Equipment

Select chargers based on your needs and electrical capacity:

  • Individual Use: Level 1 or Level 2 directly connected to resident's panel
  • Shared Use: Level 2 with Energy Management System (EVEMS) to optimize power distribution
  • Commercial/Fleet: Consider DC Fast Charging or multiple managed Level 2 stations
3

Contact Key Stakeholders Early

Engage utility and contractors before starting work:

  • NES Energy Services: 615-747-3775 or EnergyServices@NESPower.com
  • Licensed Electrical Contractor: Familiar with NES meter base specifications and trenching guidelines
  • Property Stakeholders: For HOAs/condos, consult residents and board to gauge interest

2. Electrical Capacity & Utility Upgrades

Understanding your electrical capacity is critical before adding EV chargers. Many installations require panel upgrades or new service connections, especially for Level 2 and DC Fast Charging.

Capacity Assessment Checklist

  • Main Service Panel Capacity: Check total amperage rating and compare to current load
  • Load Study: Hire a licensed electrician to calculate existing usage vs. available capacity
  • Peak Demand: Review utility bills to identify peak usage periods
  • Subpanels: Check if dedicated subpanels exist for garage/parking areas
  • Spare Breaker Slots: Verify available circuit breaker positions
  • Transformer Capacity: Contact NES to verify transformer can handle additional load

When Upgrades Are Needed

Panel Upgrades

If your main service or subpanels are near capacity, you may need to install additional panels or upgrade existing ones. NES involvement is required when upgrading from 200A to 400A or higher.

Transformer Upgrades

If the transformer serving your property is maxed out, NES may need to upgrade it. This typically requires a Contribution in Aid of Construction (CIAC) fee and can take 6-18 months.

Load Management Alternative

Smart chargers or Energy Management Systems (EVEMS) can reduce required capacity by 30-50%, potentially avoiding costly infrastructure upgrades.

3. NES Involvement & Requirements

Nashville Electric Service (NES) must be involved (or at least consulted) for most EV charging installations that impact the building's electrical service. Here's a comprehensive guide to when NES involvement is required.

Decision Matrix: When is NES Required?

üî¥ NES IS Required

  • New or Upgraded Service: Upgrading from 200A to 400A or higher, or installing a dedicated meter for EV charging
  • New Transformer: Installing DC Fast Chargers (50kW+) or multiple chargers requiring larger transformer capacity
  • New Metered Account: Adding a separate meter for EV charging (public, pay-per-use, or common area)
  • Distribution Changes: Relocating overhead lines, burying cables, or modifying NES-owned equipment
  • CT Metering: Any service above 200A requiring current transformers
  • Solar/Battery Export: Systems that can export power back to the NES grid require interconnection approval
  • Replacing Conductors: Replacing any customer-owned conductor (even with same amperage)
  • Adding Load: Changing panel size to accommodate additional electrical load

üü¢ NES NOT Required

  • Small Addition: Adding a Level 2 (30-40A) charger to existing 200A panel with adequate spare capacity
  • Subpanel Only: Adding a subpanel for EV charging without upsizing main service
  • Same-Rating Replacement: Swapping a broken 40A charger with a new 40A unit on the same circuit
  • Off-Grid Backup: Installing an open-transition generator that never parallels with NES
  • Replacing Meter Blocks: Simple meter block replacement without conductor changes
  • Same Amperage Base: Replacing meter base with same amperage and NO wire replacement
  • Conduit/Weather-head: Replacing or reattaching conduit, changing out weather-head or hub
  • Breaker Replacement: Replacing breakers within existing panel capacity

NES Release Types

Electric Release Required When:

  • Installing a new service
  • Replacing conductor with new conductor
  • Adding additional load (changing panel size)
  • Relocating a service or building new service
  • Service has been de-energized over one year

Change Meter Order Required When:

  • Adding additional load (changing panel size)
  • Relocating a service
  • Replacing an existing service
  • Replacing conductor with new conductor
  • Whenever an Electrical Release is required

4. Metro Codes & Permits

All EV charging installations in Nashville-Davidson County require permits from Metro Codes Administration. This section covers the key code requirements and permit fees.

Chapter 16.20 - Electrical Code +
  • Chapter 16.20 - Complete Electrical Code
  • ¬ß16.20.250 - Electrical Permit Fee Schedule
  • ¬ß16.20.290 - Additions to Existing Installations: No permit issued until sufficient information confirms installation can safely carry existing and added loads
  • ¬ß16.20.350 - Inspections Required: All new electrical installations must be inspected; no electric current until approved
Chapter 16.28 - Building Permits +
  • Chapter 16.28 - Complete Building Permits chapter
  • ¬ß16.28.110 - Building Permit Fee Schedule
  • ¬ß16.28.120 - Valuations & Computations: How permit fees are calculated based on project value
  • ¬ß16.28.150 - Foundations Permit & Utility Installations
  • ¬ß16.28.210 - Work Authorized: Building permit does not waive requirement for separate electrical permit
Homeowner Permits (¬ß16.20.040) +

Homeowners may install or maintain electrical systems within their own property boundaries under certain conditions:

  • Repairs/additions must be to owner's principal dwelling
  • Only one electrical permit per 12-month period
  • Work must be within homeowner's capabilities
  • Homeowner must do work themselves (no contractor assistance)
  • Proof of residency and ownership may be required
  • Not available for commercial purposes

Source: Metro Code ¬ß16.20.040

Frequently Asked Questions
What permits do I need to install an EV charger? +

At minimum, you need an electrical permit from Metro Codes ($75 minimum fee). If your installation requires a panel upgrade or new service, you'll also need an NES service release. Commercial installations may require additional building permits. See Metro Code ¬ß16.20.250 for the complete fee schedule.

What is CIAC (Contribution in Aid of Construction)? +

CIAC is a fee charged when your project requires NES to upgrade infrastructure beyond their standard allowable investment. This commonly applies when installing DC Fast Chargers or multiple Level 2 chargers that require a new or larger transformer. NES provides cost estimates during the engineering phase, and estimates are valid for 30 days. See NES Electric Service Policies ¬ß1.3 for details.

What's the difference between a Service Release and a Change Meter Order? +

An Electric Release is required for new service, replacing conductors, adding load, or relocating service. A Change Meter Order is required when modifying existing service (adding load, relocating, replacing conductors). Both require NES involvement, but a Change Meter Order applies to modifications of existing service while an Electric Release may apply to new installations.

Am I eligible for the EVC rate? +

The EVC (Electric Vehicle Charging) rate is available for commercial customers with demand between 50 kW and 5,000 kW, primarily for DC Fast Charging and fleet charging operations. Residential customers and smaller Level 2 installations typically remain on standard residential or GSA rates. See the EVC Rate Schedule (April 2025) for current rates.

How long does NES approval take? +

Timeline varies by project complexity:
‚Ä¢ Simple upgrades: 2-4 weeks
‚Ä¢ New residential service: 4-8 weeks (apply 30 days in advance)
‚Ä¢ New commercial service: 6-12 weeks (apply 60 days in advance)
‚Ä¢ Major infrastructure (new transformer): 6-18 months due to supply chain constraints
Cost estimates are valid for 30 days. Contact NES Energy Services at 615-747-3775 early in your planning process.

Can I install an EV charger myself? +

Under Metro Code ¬ß16.20.040, homeowners may perform electrical work on their principal dwelling with certain restrictions: only one electrical permit per 12 months, work must be within homeowner's capabilities, and you cannot use a contractor. A permit and inspection are still required. For panel upgrades or new circuits, consulting a licensed electrician is strongly recommended.
