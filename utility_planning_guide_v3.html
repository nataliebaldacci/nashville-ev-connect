Steps for Utility Planning
1

Assess Current Infrastructure

Evaluate your existing electrical capacity before planning any installation. Refer to NES Electric Service Guidelines v20 Table 1 for standard service ratings.

  • Check main service panel capacity (amps rating)
  • Identify distance from panel to charging location
  • Determine if existing transformer can handle additional load
  • Review any existing load studies or electrical plans
2

Contact NES Energy Services

Reach out to NES for an initial assessment. Per NES Electric Service Policies, applications should be submitted 30 days (residential) or 60 days (commercial) in advance.

  • Call: (615) 747-3775
  • Email: EnergyServices@NESPower.com
  • Discuss transformer capacity, service lines, and load impacts
  • Get cost estimates (valid for 30 days per NES Policies ¬ß1.3)
3

Submit Application & Site Plan

If NES involvement is required, submit formal application per Site Plan Requirements.

  • Complete NES Commercial Service Application
  • Provide civil site plan showing charger locations
  • Include load calculations and equipment specifications
  • Pay any required deposit or CIAC fees
4

Obtain Permits from Metro Codes

Secure all required permits per Metro Code Title 16.

  • Electrical permit: $75 minimum + per-circuit fees
  • Service release permit if upgrading service
  • Building permit if structural work required
  • Submit plans for review (may take 2-4 weeks)
5

Installation & Inspections

Complete installation following NES Guidelines Tables 5 & 9 and schedule inspections.

  • Trenching: 42" for residential, 48" for commercial
  • Use #67 gravel and meet NES backfill requirements
  • Request NES ditch inspection before backfill (if underground)
  • Schedule Metro Codes electrical inspection per ¬ß16.20.350
6

NES Service Release & Energization

Final steps per NES Guidelines ¬ß6.2 and Metro Code ¬ß16.04.100.

  • NES requires electrical release from Metro Codes
  • NES installs/swaps meter after inspection passes
  • Service is energized and chargers activated
  • Set up billing account (EVC rate if eligible)

When is NES Involvement Required?

The need for NES involvement depends on whether your installation requires changes to the utility service or meter. Here's a quick decision guide:

üî¥ NES IS Required

  • New or Upgraded Service: Upgrading from 200A to 400A or higher for EV chargers
  • New Transformer: Installing DC Fast Chargers (50kW+) or multiple chargers requiring larger transformer
  • New Metered Account: Adding a separate meter for EV charging (public or pay-per-use)
  • Distribution Changes: Relocating overhead lines or burying cables for EV canopy
  • CT Metering: Any service above 200A requiring current transformers
  • Solar/Battery Export: Systems that can export power back to the grid (per Guidelines Ch. VI)

üü¢ NES NOT Required

  • Small Addition: Adding a Level 2 (30-40A) charger to existing 200A panel with spare capacity
  • Subpanel Only: Adding a subpanel for EV charging without upsizing main service
  • Same-Rating Replacement: Swapping a broken 40A charger with a new 40A unit
  • Off-Grid Backup: Installing an open-transition generator that never parallels with NES

üìñ NES Policy References

  • NES Electric Service Policies - ¬ß1.9: Service requirements and CIAC fees
  • NES Rules & Regulations - ¬ß23: No load additions without NES consent; customer liable for damage
  • NES Electric Service Guidelines v20 - Complete technical requirements
  • NES Customer Relations Policies - Customer service and billing policies

‚ö° Electric Release Required When:

  • Installing a new service
  • Replacing conductor with new conductor
  • Adding additional load (changing panel size)
  • Relocating a service or building new service
  • Service has been de-energized over one year

üîÑ Change Meter Order Required When:

  • Adding additional load (changing panel size)
  • Relocating a service
  • Replacing an existing service
  • Replacing conductor with new conductor
  • Whenever an Electrical Release is required

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Multifamily is Commercial

Under NES Electric Service Guidelines, multifamily buildings (condos, apartments) are classified as commercial service, even if residents are using chargers for personal vehicles. Three-phase service is also considered commercial. This affects rates, metering requirements, and service classifications.

NES EV Charging Rates

NES offers special rate schedules for EV charging. Rates are regulated by the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA). The EVC rate is designed for commercial EV charging installations.

Rate Schedule Demand Range Best For Details
EVC (Electric Vehicle Charging) Greater than 50 kW but not more than 5,000 kW DC Fast Chargers, Fleet Charging Lower demand charges for EV loads
GSA (General Power) Up to 1,000 kW Small Commercial, Multifamily Standard commercial rate
TDGSA (Time-of-Day General) Greater than 1,000 kW but not more than 5,000 kW Large facilities with load flexibility Lower off-peak rates
TDMSA (Time-of-Day Manufacturing) Greater than 1,000 kW but not more than 5,000 kW with SIC code 20-39 Manufacturing facilities Special manufacturing rate
Residential N/A Single-family homes Standard tiered residential rate

üí° EVC Rate Eligibility

The EVC rate requires demand greater than 50 kW. Most single Level 2 chargers won't qualify (typical Level 2 is 7-19 kW). Multi-charger commercial installations or DC fast chargers may be eligible. Contact NES to discuss rate options.

üìù Homeowner Permits (¬ß16.20.040)

Nashville allows homeowners to obtain permits for electrical work on their principal dwelling, but only if: (1) the work is within average DIY capability, (2) the homeowner does the work themselves without contractor help, (3) only one electrical permit is issued within a 12-month period, and (4) the work is not for commercial purposes.

Frequently Asked Questions
What permits are needed for EV charger installation? +

At minimum, you need an electrical permit ($75 minimum). If you're upgrading your service, you'll also need a service release permit ($75 residential, $102 commercial). Building permits may be required if structural work is needed.

What is a Contribution in Aid of Construction (CIAC)? +

CIAC is a fee charged when NES infrastructure upgrades (transformers, conductors, etc.) are required to serve your installation. NES provides cost estimates during the engineering phase. This fee covers the portion of infrastructure costs that exceed NES's standard allowable investment.

How long does the NES approval process take? +

Timeline varies based on complexity. Applications should be submitted 30 days in advance for residential, 60 days for commercial. Simple service upgrades may take 2-4 weeks. Projects requiring new transformers can take 6-18 months due to supply chain delays.

Can I use the EVC rate for my condo charging station? +

The EVC rate is available for installations with demand between 50 kW and 5,000 kW. Most single Level 2 chargers won't qualify, but larger installations with multiple chargers or DC fast chargers may be eligible. Contact NES to discuss rate options.

What is a service release vs. a change meter order? +

Service Release: Required for NEW services or reconnecting de-energized services (over 1 year). Involves full NES engineering and Codes inspection.

Change Meter Order: Required when upgrading existing service (panel size change, conductor replacement, relocation). Also requires Codes release.

What about supply chain delays? +

NES is currently experiencing significant supply chain delays for transformers, poles, and meters. Plan for 12-18 month lead times on major infrastructure. Consider the "Bring Your Own Transformer" policy for DC Fast Charger installations to potentially reduce delays.
